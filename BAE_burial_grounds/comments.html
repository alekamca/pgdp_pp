The BAE Returns

The Bureau of Ethnology—later the Bureau of American Ethnology—published a total of 48 Annual Reports over the period 1879 to 1933. (1919-24 was a long year, and most reports were published a year or more after their stated date.) Each Report was divided into three pieces: introductory material including summaries of what everyone in the Bureau had been doing; a number of free-standing articles; and an Index covering the whole volume. In later years, the individual articles got longer and fewer. Some reports have only a single book-length article.

At DP, each article is a separate project, and the introduction and index are combined into another project. Articles are posted separately to PG, and when a volume is finished, a whole-volume version is also posted.

For more information, see DP’s BAE wiki page.

The Short Version

The Retread Notes apply to all rounds. A few additional notes are specific to Proofing or Formatting.
RETREAD NOTES: All Rounds
Small Capitals

The <sc> markup did not exist, so mixed-case SMALL CAPS were generally proofed as ALL CAPS. Proofers should leave them as you find them; formatters, keep your eyes peeled.
Asterisks

Proofers used asterisks * liberally to mark illegible words or letters. Please get rid of these and, if appropriate, replace them with full-format [**notes].
Tables

Some BAE tables were laid out using a LaTeX-style markup. This is no longer in use, so you may get rid of the extra symbols if they distract you.
Moved Text

Illustration captions, footnote continuations and similar blocks of text may have been moved to unexpected places.

Proofers: Don’t relocate this text. It isn’t bothering anyone, and moving it plays havoc with the Diff display. Leave it for the Formatters.

Formatters: Put any relocated text where current Guidelines say it belongs, and make sure any asterisks—such as for continued footnotes—are in the right places.
PROOFING ISSUES
Potential Problems

As with all 2-round projects, be especially alert for proofing errors in the immediate vicinity of inline formatting markup. One approach is to first check these areas, and then clear all formatting (using the [X] button) and re-proof the whole page. Please do not feel bad about deleting formatting; much of it is so old that the Formatters will probably find it easier to throw it out and start from scratch.
Diacritics and Other Unusual Characters

For information on proofing unusual characters, see the BAE Proofing Wiki. The overall principles are the same as the current Guidelines.
Apostrophes

Proof the three different types of apostrophes using this markup:

'   = normal apostrophe, printed ’ (curls to left = single close quote)
`   = backwards apostrophe representing glottal stop, printed ‛ (curls to right, similar to single open quote)
['] = simple line showing pronunciation, printed ' or ´

For example, the word

would be proofed as
Ä[']-t'a`ka[']-i

FORMATTING ISSUES

Your job is to bring the formatting of this project—dating from 2005 or earlier—into line with current guidelines. If the proofers chose not to delete inline markup, you can either work with it or delete it yourself and start from scratch.
Punctuation and Inline Markup

Make sure all closing tags are in the right place. Closing markup always comes after the period in abbreviations, such as <i>etc.</i> Elsewhere, closing markup usually comes after periods and similar sentence-ending punctuation; before other punctuation. If there is an apparent conflict between your own common sense and other generic guidelines—including these—use your common sense.
Small Caps

When this project went through the rounds, <sc> markup did not exist. Mixed-case SMALL CAPS were generally proofed as ALL CAPS, and SINGLE-SIZE SMALL CAPS were not marked at all.

As with all small-caps markup, Keep it simple. The small-caps property has no effect on anything but lower-case letters, so

FIG. 1.
    can be tagged as
<sc>Fig. 1.</sc>

and

JOHN WESLEY POWELL
    can be
<sc>John Wesley POWELL</sc>
